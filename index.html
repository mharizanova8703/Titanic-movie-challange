Chapter II — April 10, 1912 · Southampton

Jack Meets Rose

The real Titanic left Southampton at noon with 2,224 souls aboard — millionaires and emigrants, and a crew of nearly nine hundred.

In the film, one of them is Rose DeWitt Bukater — seventeen, first class, engaged to a man she does not love. Another is Jack Dawson, a penniless artist who wins his ticket in a poker game five minutes before she sails.

Two nights out, she runs for the stern rail. He talks her back from the edge.

“You jump, I jump.”

Chapter III — April 14 · The Last Sunset

Falling in Love

By the fourth day the Atlantic lay flat as glass, and the ship had sailed through six separate ice warnings without slowing.

At sunset Jack takes Rose to the bow, where the ocean has no edges. She steps onto the rail, closes her eyes, and opens her arms.

“I'm flying, Jack.”

That evening she chooses him — third class, penniless, alive — over everything she was born into.

Chapter IV — April 14 · 11:40 p.m. · 28°F

Iceberg, Right Ahead

37seconds from sighting to steel

High in the crow's nest, lookout Frederick Fleet had no binoculars — the key to their locker had left the ship in Southampton.

The ice opened her starboard side along six of her sixteen compartments. She was built to survive four.

“She's made of iron, sir. I assure you, she can sink. And she will.”

Chapter V — April 15 · 2:20 a.m.

Never Let Go

The real ship broke in two and fell twelve thousand feet. Over fifteen hundred people went into water cold enough to kill in minutes — and the half-empty boats rowed back for six.

In the film, a floating door holds only one. Jack keeps Rose on it, and makes her promise him the rest of her life.

“I'll never let go, Jack. I promise.”

At dawn the Carpathia lifted 706 survivors from the sea. Rose Dawson was among them.

The ocean is calm now.

“A woman's heart is a deep ocean of secrets.”

Epilogue — 84 Years Later

The Heart of the Ocean

Cal's engagement gift to Rose was a deep blue diamond cut for a king — worth more, he said, than the ship that carried it. She was wearing it the night Jack drew her; she found it in the pocket of his coat on the deck of the Carpathia, and told no one for eighty-four years.

In 1985, seventy-three years after the sinking, Robert Ballard's cameras found the real Titanic standing upright in the dark, two and a half miles down — bow dignified, stern in ruin. In the film, an old woman sails out to the discovery, walks to the rail one last night, and lets the diamond fall — back to the ship, back to him.

The sea keeps her secrets gently.
